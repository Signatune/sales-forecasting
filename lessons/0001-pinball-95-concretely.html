Lesson 01 · Reading the model score

How pinball@95 is calculated, concretely

The whole model bake-off comes down to one number per model. By the end of this page you can compute it by hand for a week of bagels — and say why a Stockout hurts it ~19× harder than a leftover.

1 · What is being scored

Each model turns its point forecast into a P95 Poolish quantity — the buffered amount that’s supposed to cover Demand 95% of the time (Service Level). Pinball scores that quantity, day by day, against the actual Wheat Dough Demand that showed up.

So for one day you always have exactly two numbers: actual (what Demand turned out to be) and forecast (the model’s P95 quantity). Everything below is just what we do with those two.

2 · The formula (one day)

Let diff = actual − forecast, and the level be 0.95.

loss = 0.95 × (actual − forecast)  if actual ≥ forecast  (under-forecast → Stockout)
loss = 0.05 × (forecast − actual)  if actual < forecast  (over-forecast → leftover)

That’s the entire idea. Whichever way you missed, the loss is the size of the miss times a weight. The only trick is that the two directions get different weights: 0.95 when you baked too little, 0.05 when you baked too much.

Why those weights = the 19× asymmetry 0.95 ÷ 0.05 = 19. A day you’re 10 bagels short costs 0.95×10 = 9.5. A day you’re 10 bagels over costs 0.05×10 = 0.5. Same 10-bagel miss, but the Stockout is scored 19× heavier — because a lost sale is treated as far costlier than a leftover. That asymmetry is the whole reason we use this metric instead of plain error (MAE/MAPE), which would score both misses the same.
Heads-up when you read the textbook The FPP3 text (§5.9) writes this with a factor of 2 — 2p and 2(1−p) — so its numbers are exactly double ours. Same shape, same 19:1 ratio, identical model rankings. Our code just drops the 2.

3 · A week, worked by hand

One model’s P95 quantity vs. actual Demand over five bake days. Follow the last column — red = short (Stockout), green = over (leftover).

mean6.94

Sum of daily losses = 0.50 + 14.25 + 0.75 + 0.20 + 19.00 = 34.70. Mean pinball@95 = 34.70 ÷ 5 = 6.94. That mean is the model’s headline score — lower is better.

Look what drove it: three of five days were over-baked, yet the score is dominated by the two short days (14.25 + 19.00 = 33.25 of the 34.70). One busy Friday moves this number more than a whole quiet week. Hold that thought — it’s why a 5%-lower score can be noise, not evidence, which is a later lesson.

Companion number: coverage This model covered Demand on Mon/Wed/Thu = 3 of 5 days = 60%, well under its 95% promise. Pinball says how costly the misses were; coverage says how often they happened (model_comparison.py:288).
